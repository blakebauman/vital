Turn existing customers into the most credible growth engine the client has: proof that closes new deals, references that unblock enterprise buyers, and advocacy that compounds. Customer marketing starts after there are customers with real outcomes — confirm the client has willing, successful customers before building programs. Anchor to the client's segment — see `${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/skills/vital-playbook/references/segments.md`.

## 1. Identify the proof

Map the customers worth featuring: who got a real, quantifiable outcome, who is willing to go public, and who represents an ICP the client wants more of. Prioritize stories that mirror the target buyer — a prospect believes a customer who looks like them. For each candidate note the outcome, the metric, the segment fit, and the reference willingness (logo only, quote, full story, live reference call).

## 2. Case studies

Build case studies in the proven structure: the customer and their context, the problem/stakes, why they chose this product (the buying trigger and alternatives considered), what they did, and the quantified result. Lead with the result. Keep the customer the hero, not the product. Draft interview questions to extract the story, then the written case study, plus short-form cutdowns (one-line quote, social card, sales one-pager). Route visuals to `design-brief`. Never fabricate quotes, metrics, or logos — everything traces to a real customer who approved it.

## 3. Testimonials & social proof

Collect and place testimonials where they convert: pricing page, landing pages, checkout, sales decks. Design a lightweight system to capture them (post-onboarding, post-win, NPS promoters) and a bank the client can reuse. Match the testimonial to the objection it answers at each page.

## 4. Reviews (review-site presence)

For categories where buyers check review sites (G2, Capterra, TrustRadius, app stores), design a review-generation program: which sites matter for this ICP and segment, when and how to ask (trigger on a success moment, never incentivize a *positive* review specifically), and how to respond to reviews. Research the client's current presence and the category leaders with web search; cite what you find. Reviews are enterprise trust signals and SEO surface, so treat them as an ongoing program, not a one-time push.

## 5. Reference program

For enterprise motions, stand up a reference program: a managed pool of customers willing to take reference calls, the matching logic (match reference to prospect by industry/size/use case), incentives and reciprocity (early access, advisory board, co-marketing), and guardrails so top references are not overused. References unblock late-stage enterprise deals that proof alone cannot.

## 6. Advocacy & community

Design how advocates amplify beyond a single asset: user community, champion/ambassador program, co-marketing (joint webinars, guest content), speaking and awards, and referral loops. Tie advocacy to a growth loop where advocates bring the next customers.

## 7. Expansion & retention marketing

Market to existing customers to drive expansion and retention: onboarding/adoption content, feature-launch communication to the base, upsell/cross-sell campaigns to the right accounts, and win-back for churned users. Coordinate with `content-engine` for lifecycle email and with `abm-playbook` for expansion within named accounts.

## Segment notes

- **Enterprise**: reference calls, logo rights, analyst-grade case studies, and executive advocacy carry the most weight; the reference program is often deal-critical.
- **Mid-market**: written case studies, review-site presence, and ROI proof drive the shortlist.
- **Small business**: volume of reviews and testimonials, quick social proof, and community/referral loops matter more than bespoke references.

## Output

Deliver: the proof map, drafted case studies (with interview questions) and cutdowns, a testimonial-capture system and bank, a review-generation program, an enterprise reference program where relevant, an advocacy/community plan, and expansion/retention campaigns. Keep every claim traceable to a real, consenting customer — no invented quotes, metrics, or logos, and respect review-site rules. Include a Sources section for review research. Save the assets and programs to the project.
