# Client segments

Vital serves three client tiers. **Enterprise and mid-market are the primary focus** — that is where Vital does its best, most valuable work. **Small businesses are fully supported as a secondary tier**: helped to succeed with a leaner, faster version of the same rigor.

Always establish the client's segment during intake, because it changes the motion, the stakeholders, the timeline, and the deliverables across every discipline. When a client spans tiers (e.g., a mid-market company selling to enterprise), tier by the buyer they are trying to win, not by their own size.

## Segment definitions

| Tier | Rough shape | Buying reality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Enterprise** (primary) | 1,000+ employees, or selling into them | Buying committee (5–10+ stakeholders), procurement, security/legal review, budget cycles, long sales cycle (3–12mo), high ACV |
| **Mid-market** (primary) | ~100–1,000 employees | A few stakeholders, some process, moderate cycle (1–3mo), hybrid self-serve + sales |
| **Small business** (supported) | < ~100 employees, SMB/solo | One or two decision-makers, fast decision, low friction, self-serve, price-sensitive |

## How each discipline adapts

### Strategy / positioning
- **Enterprise**: separate the *user*, the *economic buyer*, and the *champion* — map the buying committee and each one's concern (ROI, risk, control, adoption). Position against "build vs. buy" and incumbent suites. Lead with risk reduction, security, integration, and total cost, not just speed.
- **Mid-market**: a primary buyer who is also a heavy user, usually with a small committee (2–3, e.g. a budget owner and a security/IT check) to satisfy — center the primary buyer but map the others when they can block. Position on time-to-value and doing more without more headcount.
- **Small business**: one persona, one job. Position on immediate, tangible outcome and low effort. No committee — remove every reason to hesitate.

### GTM
- **Enterprise**: sales-led, often ABM (account-based) targeting named accounts. Design partners and lighthouse logos first. Expect pilots/POCs, MSAs, security questionnaires (SOC 2, SSO/SAML), and a longer, phased launch. Pricing is quote/tiered with annual contracts; value metric tied to seats/usage/outcomes.
- **Mid-market**: hybrid — self-serve entry with a sales-assist motion for expansion. Land-and-expand. Transparent tiered pricing with an option to talk to sales.
- **Small business**: product-led and self-serve. Low/no-touch onboarding, transparent pricing, monthly option, fast time-to-value. Volume over ACV; churn is the enemy.

### Brand / messaging
- **Enterprise**: credible, proof-heavy, low-hype. Message hierarchy needs security, compliance, integration, and enterprise-readiness pillars with hard proof (certifications, customer logos, uptime, case studies). Voice: confident and precise, never gimmicky.
- **Mid-market**: benefit-forward with proof close behind. Approachable but substantiated.
- **Small business**: plain, direct, outcome-first. Minimal jargon. Voice can be warmer and more personable.

### Design
- **Enterprise**: polished, trustworthy, "we won't get you fired" cues — clean, restrained, enterprise-grade. Assets include stakeholder-ready decks, one-pagers for procurement/security, and ROI visuals.
- **Mid-market**: modern and confident; balance polish with personality.
- **Small business**: bold, fast to grasp, conversion-focused. Fewer, sharper assets.

### Marketing / content
- **Enterprise**: thought leadership, analyst-grade content, webinars, whitepapers, case studies, and account-based campaigns to named targets. Longer nurture; content maps to a multi-stakeholder committee.
- **Mid-market**: comparison content, ROI calculators, product-led content, and lifecycle email. Nurture measured in weeks.
- **Small business**: high-velocity, top-of-funnel, SEO and social, self-serve education, and templates. Fast loops.

### PR / comms
- **Enterprise**: analyst relations matter (Gartner, Forrester, IDC — briefings, Magic Quadrant/Wave). Trade and vertical press, customer proof, and executive thought leadership. Announcements emphasize enterprise credibility and named customers.
- **Mid-market**: tech and vertical press, launch platforms, founder narrative, and customer stories.
- **Small business**: launch platforms (Product Hunt, communities), niche newsletters, and word-of-mouth. Scrappy earned attention over analyst relations.

## Rule of thumb
Match the rigor to the buyer's risk. The higher the ACV and the more people who must say yes, the more proof, process, and patience the launch requires. Never make a small business run an enterprise process — meet each client where the buying decision actually happens.
