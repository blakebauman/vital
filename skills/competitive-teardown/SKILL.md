Produce a competitive teardown that reveals where the client can win, not just a feature matrix. Research real competitors using web search; do not invent details.

## 1. Define the set

List competitors across three rings:

- **Direct** — products solving the same problem for the same buyer.
- **Adjacent** — tools the customer might use instead, or expand from.
- **Status quo** — the manual process, spreadsheet, or "do nothing" that is often the real competitor.

## 2. Research each competitor

For each, gather from their site and public sources: positioning/tagline, target customer, category they claim, pricing and packaging, primary message and proof points, apparent strengths, and visible weaknesses or gaps. Cite sources. Flag anything you could not verify rather than guessing.

## 3. Positioning map

Place the competitors on the two axes that matter most for this market (choose axes that expose an opening — e.g., simple↔powerful, self-serve↔high-touch, general↔specialized). Show where the white space is.

## 4. Message & pricing comparison

Summarize how rivals talk about the problem (the shared clichés to avoid) and their pricing models and anchor points. Note where everyone sounds the same — that sameness is an opening for a distinct voice.

## 5. The wedge

State the specific, defensible angle the client can own: an underserved segment, an unmet job, a pricing model, or a message no one else is making. Be honest if the market is genuinely crowded and the wedge is narrow.

## 6. Watch-outs

Note the strongest competitor's likely response, and any moats (distribution, data, integrations) that will be hard to overcome.

## Output

Deliver: the competitive set by ring, a per-competitor breakdown with sources, a positioning map, a message/pricing comparison, the named wedge, and watch-outs. Feed the wedge back into the `positioning` skill. Save findings to the project. Include a Sources section citing every competitor page referenced.
