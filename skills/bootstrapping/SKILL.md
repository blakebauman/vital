Help a founder build a strong company without giving up equity — deliberately, not as a fallback. Bootstrapping trades speed for control and optionality; done well it produces a durable, profitable business and keeps every future path open (including raising later from strength).

> **Not financial, tax, or investment advice.** Vital provides frameworks and information only. Financing choices (including debt and revenue-based financing) have binding financial and tax consequences. Tell the founder to consult a qualified accountant/advisor before taking on financing or making major financial commitments.

## 1. Is bootstrapping the right path?

Confirm the fit. Bootstrapping suits businesses that can reach revenue quickly, have reasonable capital needs, serve a market that may not support a venture-scale outcome, or whose founders value control and profitability over maximum speed. If the company must win a large market before well-funded competitors and needs capital to do it, revisit the `fundraising` skill. The goal is a deliberate choice, not raising-by-failure or bootstrapping-by-default.

## 2. Default alive

Anchor on the core discipline: be **default alive** — on current growth and burn, does the company reach profitability before it runs out of money? Model it. If default dead, the plan is to reach default alive by growing revenue or cutting burn, not by assuming a future raise. Track runway (months of cash at current burn) as the primary survival metric.

## 3. Unit economics & capital efficiency

- **Unit economics** — establish that each customer is profitable: gross margin, CAC, payback period, LTV. Bootstrapped growth compounds only if the economics work per customer.
- **Capital efficiency** — grow from cash flow: keep burn low, hire behind revenue, prefer variable to fixed costs, and reinvest profit into the highest-return growth loops. Prioritize the few things that move revenue.
- **Pricing** — pricing is the fastest lever for a bootstrapped company; coordinate with the `pricing-strategy` skill (charge for value, raise prices as you prove it).

## 4. Customer-funded growth

The strongest bootstrapping fuel is customers. Design for it: get to revenue early, use annual prepay for cash flow, pre-sell before building where possible, and let a compounding growth loop (content, referral, product-led) reduce paid acquisition dependence. Coordinate with `gtm-plan` (product-led motion), `content-engine`, and `community-building`.

## 5. Non-dilutive funding options

When outside capital helps but equity isn't the right price, cover the non-dilutive menu and its trade-offs:

- **Revenue / profit** — the cleanest capital; no cost but time.
- **Grants** — non-dilutive, non-repayable (e.g., government innovation grants, SBIR/STTR in the US, foundation grants for mission-aligned work). Slow but free.
- **Crowdfunding** — reward-based (Kickstarter/Indiegogo) doubles as pre-sales and demand validation; equity crowdfunding is dilutive and regulated.
- **Revenue-based financing** — capital repaid as a % of revenue (e.g., for SaaS with predictable MRR). Non-dilutive but has a real cost; model it.
- **Venture debt / bank debt / lines of credit** — non-dilutive but requires repayment and often covenants; riskier for pre-revenue.
- **Customer pre-sales / deposits** — the customer funds the build. Strong signal and non-dilutive.

Recommend based on stage, revenue predictability, and risk tolerance; flag the cost and obligations of anything repayable, and route financing decisions to an advisor.

## 6. When (and how) to raise later

Bootstrapping and raising are not mutually exclusive forever. Note the option to raise later from a position of strength (revenue, traction, leverage) at a better valuation and on the founder's terms — or to stay independent indefinitely. Keep the structure clean so the option stays open.
